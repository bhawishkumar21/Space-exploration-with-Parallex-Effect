1. Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in our solar system, orbiting closest to the Sun at a distance of about 57.9 million kilometers. Despite its proximity to the Sun, it isn’t the hottest planet—Venus holds that title due to its thick atmosphere. Mercury has a very thin atmosphere, called an exosphere, which cannot trap heat, causing extreme temperature variations between day and night. The planet completes an orbit around the Sun in just 88 Earth days, but due to its slow rotation, one full day (from sunrise to sunrise) lasts about 176 Earth days. Its surface is rocky and heavily cratered, resembling our Moon, and it has a large iron core that contributes to a weak magnetic field. Mercury has no moons and has been explored by NASA’s Mariner 10 and MESSENGER missions, with the European-Japanese BepiColombo mission currently on its way for further study.

ORBIT: 87 Earth Days
MOONS: None

2. Venus

Venus is the second planet in our solar system and is often called Earth's "sister planet" because of its similar size and composition. However, it has a thick atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid, making it the hottest planet—even hotter than Mercury—reaching surface temperatures of around 465°C (869°F). Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. One day on Venus (one full rotation) takes about 243 Earth days, while a year is only 225 Earth days. Its surface is rocky and volcanic, hidden beneath dense clouds that trap heat through a strong greenhouse effect. Despite being inhospitable, Venus has been studied by many missions, including NASA’s Magellan and Europe’s Venus Express, revealing a fascinating but extreme environment.

ORBIT: 224 Earth days
KNOWN MOONS: None

3. Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life. It has a balanced atmosphere made up mostly of nitrogen and oxygen, which helps regulate temperature and allows living organisms to breathe. About 71% of Earth’s surface is covered by water, making it unique among the planets. Earth has a diameter of about 12,742 kilometers and takes 365.25 days to orbit the Sun, creating one year. It rotates once every 24 hours, giving us day and night. Earth's magnetic field and atmosphere protect it from harmful solar radiation and meteoroids. It has one natural satellite, the Moon, which affects ocean tides and stabilizes the planet's tilt. With a diverse climate and rich ecosystems, Earth is home to millions of species and continues to be a dynamic and ever-changing world.

ORBIT: 365 days
KNOWN MOONS: 1

4. Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is often called the "Red Planet" because of its reddish appearance, which comes from iron oxide (rust) on its surface. It is a cold, rocky planet with a thin atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide. Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and a huge canyon called Valles Marineris. A day on Mars, called a "sol," is just a little over 24 hours, and it takes 687 Earth days to complete one orbit around the Sun. Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos. Scientists are very interested in Mars because it once had liquid water and might have supported microbial life. Many missions, like NASA’s Perseverance rover, are exploring the planet to learn more about its past and whether humans could live there in the future.

ORBIT: 686 Earth days
MOONS: 2

5. Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest planet in our solar system. It is a gas giant, made mostly of hydrogen and helium, and has no solid surface. Jupiter is famous for its Great Red Spot, a giant storm larger than Earth that has been raging for centuries. It has a very strong magnetic field and at least 95 known moons, including Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system. Jupiter rotates very quickly—one day lasts just about 10 hours—but it takes about 12 Earth years to orbit the Sun. The planet has colorful bands and swirling clouds caused by strong winds and storms in its atmosphere. Because of its massive size and strong gravity, Jupiter plays an important role in protecting the inner planets by pulling in comets and asteroids that might otherwise hit Earth.

ORBIT: 4,332 Earth days
MOONS: 67

6. Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest planet in the solar system, known for its stunning and complex ring system. These rings are made of ice, rock, and dust particles and are one of the most beautiful features in the solar system. Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface. It has a fast rotation—one day lasts about 10.7 hours—and it takes about 29.5 Earth years to complete one orbit around the Sun. Saturn has over 140 known moons, including Titan, which is larger than Mercury and has a thick atmosphere. Despite its large size, Saturn is the least dense planet—it would float in water if a big enough ocean existed! Scientists continue to study Saturn to understand more about its atmosphere, rings, and moons.

ORBIT PERIOD: 10,755 Earth days
MOONS: 62

7. Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and is known for its unique sideways rotation—it spins on its side, with an axial tilt of about 98 degrees. This makes its seasons extremely long and unusual. Uranus is an ice giant, made mostly of icy materials like water, methane, and ammonia, along with hydrogen and helium. The methane in its atmosphere gives the planet its bluish-green color. It is very cold, with temperatures dropping to around -224°C (-371°F), making it one of the coldest planets in the solar system. Uranus takes about 17 hours to complete a day and 84 Earth years to orbit the Sun. It has 13 faint rings and 27 known moons, named mostly after characters from the works of Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. Uranus is mysterious and still not fully understood, as it has only been visited once—by NASA’s Voyager 2 in 1986.

ORBIT: 30,687 Earth days
MOONS: 27

8. Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun in our solar system. It is a cold, dark ice giant, known for its deep blue color, which comes from methane in its atmosphere. Neptune is famous for having the strongest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of over 2,000 km/h (1,200 mph). It has a thick atmosphere made of hydrogen, helium, and methane, and it experiences giant storms, including one known as the Great Dark Spot, similar to Jupiter’s Great Red Spot. A day on Neptune lasts about 16 hours, while it takes about 165 Earth years to complete one orbit around the Sun. Neptune has 14 known moons, with Triton being the largest—it orbits the planet in the opposite direction, suggesting it may have been captured. The planet was discovered in 1846 and has only been visited by Voyager 2, which flew by in 1989.

ORBIT: 60,190 Earth days
MOONS: 14

9. Pluto

Pluto is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, a region beyond Neptune filled with icy bodies and remnants from the early solar system. Once considered the ninth planet, Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union in 2006 because it does not clear its orbit of other debris. Pluto is small—about one-sixth the width of Earth—and has a rocky, icy surface with mountains, valleys, and possible underground oceans. It has a thin atmosphere made mostly of nitrogen, which expands when it’s closer to the Sun and freezes when it moves farther away. A year on Pluto is very long—it takes about 248 Earth years to orbit the Sun, and one day lasts about 6.4 Earth days. Pluto has five known moons, the largest being Charon, which is so big compared to Pluto that they’re often called a double dwarf planet system. NASA’s New Horizons mission provided the first close-up images of Pluto in 2015, revealing a surprisingly active and complex world.

ORBIT: 90,582 Earth days
MOONS: 5
